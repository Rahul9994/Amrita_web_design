Survival Kit

The do's, don'ts, and life lessons from seniors who've been there, done that. Learn from their mistakes so you don't repeat them.

Do's & Don'ts Every Fresher Must Know

Print this and stick it on your wall. Seriously.

DO's

  • Attend every class — attendance is everything
  • Carry your ID card at all times
  • Introduce yourself to professors
  • Join at least 2 clubs in your first month
  • Make friends from different branches
  • Start preparing for placements from Year 1
  • Use the library — it's a goldmine
  • Participate in at least one hackathon per year
  • Keep your room and study area organized
  • Call your parents regularly — they miss you
  • Eat healthy and exercise regularly
  • Ask for help when you need it
  • Back up your project files on cloud storage
  • Network with seniors and alumni
  • Explore Amaravati city on weekends

DON'Ts

  • Don't skip classes for sleep — you'll regret it
  • Don't copy assignments — plagiarism is serious
  • Don't stay in your room all day
  • Don't ignore mental health struggles
  • Don't spend all your money on food delivery
  • Don't get into unnecessary arguments
  • Don't procrastinate on projects till last minute
  • Don't compare yourself to others
  • Don't trust everyone blindly
  • Don't use phones during lab sessions
  • Don't miss club intro sessions — they're important
  • Don't neglect your health for grades
  • Don't break hostel rules — wardens are strict
  • Don't spread rumors or gossip
  • Don't forget to enjoy college life!

Time Management: Studies vs Activities

The ultimate balancing act. Here's how to nail it.

The 60-30-10 Rule

60% of your time on academics (classes, assignments, self-study). 30% on extracurriculars (clubs, sports, events). 10% on relaxation and socializing. This ratio keeps you balanced and productive.

Use a Planner

Whether it's a physical planner or Google Calendar, plan your week every Sunday. Block time for classes, study sessions, club meetings, and personal time. Stick to the plan!

Pomodoro Technique

Study for 25 minutes, take a 5-minute break. After 4 cycles, take a 15-minute break. This keeps your brain fresh and prevents burnout. Perfect for long study sessions.

Prioritize Ruthlessly

Not everything is urgent. Use the Eisenhower Matrix: Urgent & Important (do first), Important but Not Urgent (schedule), Urgent but Not Important (delegate), Neither (eliminate).

Sleep is Non-Negotiable

Pulling all-nighters seems cool but destroys your productivity. Aim for 7-8 hours of sleep. A well-rested brain learns faster and retains information better.

Limit Social Media

Social media is the biggest time thief. Use apps like Forest or StayFocusd to limit screen time. Your future self will thank you for every hour saved.

Mental Health & Stress Management

Your mental health is just as important as your grades. Take care of yourself.

Talk to Someone

Feeling overwhelmed? Talk to a friend, senior, counselor, or professor. Amrita has counseling services. Bottling up emotions makes things worse. You're not alone.

Exercise Regularly

Physical activity is the best stress buster. Use the sports complex, go for morning runs, or join the gym. Even 30 minutes of walking helps clear your mind.

Find Your Outlet

Music, art, writing, gaming, or whatever makes you happy. Dedicate time to hobbies. College life is stressful — you need healthy outlets.

Sleep Well

Sleep deprivation leads to anxiety, poor concentration, and health issues. Maintain a sleep schedule. Your brain needs rest to function at its best.

Meditate & Breathe

Even 5 minutes of meditation daily can reduce stress significantly. Use apps like Headspace or Calm. Deep breathing exercises work wonders before exams.

Stay Connected

Call your family regularly. Talk to old friends. Homesickness is real and normal. Staying connected to your support system keeps you grounded.

Emergency Help

If you're experiencing severe stress, anxiety, or depression, reach out immediately. Talk to the college counselor, your warden, or a trusted professor. There is NO shame in asking for help. Your well-being comes first, always.
